# The Black Forest: Germany's Great Green Heart

The name alone conjures something primal. Schwarzwald. The Black Forest. It sounds like the opening line of a fairy tale, and in a sense it is. This vast swathe of southwestern Germany, stretching 160 kilometers from Pforzheim in the north to Waldshut on the Swiss border, has been inspiring stories since long before the Brothers Grimm wandered its paths. Dense stands of spruce and fir climb steep valleys where waterfalls thunder over mossy rock. Farmhouses with impossibly wide, low-swooping roofs dot high meadows where cows wear bells that ring across entire valleys. Clockmakers still bend over workbenches in village workshops, coaxing mechanical birds from blocks of linden wood.

But the Black Forest is no relic. It is a living, working landscape where tradition and modernity exist in easy balance. Freiburg, its unofficial capital, is one of Germany's most progressive and sustainable cities. Baden-Baden draws a cosmopolitan crowd to its thermal baths and cultural festivals. And the trail network, one of the most extensive in Europe, draws hikers and trail runners from around the world. This is a region that rewards slow travel, that asks you to put away the checklist and simply walk deeper into the trees.

## Freiburg im Breisgau: The Sunny Gateway

Any exploration of the Black Forest should begin in Freiburg, a university city of 230,000 nestled at the western edge of the forest where the Rhine plain meets the hills. With more sunshine hours than almost anywhere else in Germany and a medieval Altstadt (old town) laced with narrow water channels called Bächle, Freiburg has the relaxed, sun-warmed energy of a southern European city that somehow ended up in Baden-Württemberg.

The **Freiburger Münster** (cathedral) dominates the old town with its lace-like Gothic spire, widely considered the most beautiful church tower in Christendom. Climb the 333 steps to the viewing platform (3 EUR) for a panorama stretching from the Vosges Mountains in France to the forested ridges of the Black Forest. Below, the Münsterplatz hosts a daily market where vendors sell everything from local Spargel (asparagus) in spring to Glühwein (mulled wine) in December. Do not leave the market without trying a Lange Rote, Freiburg's signature extra-long red sausage served in a crusty roll with onions and mustard (3.50 EUR).

The **Augustinermuseum** (7 EUR) houses an exceptional collection of medieval art, including original stone figures from the cathedral and luminous altarpieces from Black Forest monasteries. For contemporary culture, cross the river to the **Vauban district**, a car-free neighborhood that functions as a living laboratory for sustainable urban design, with green rooftops, solar-powered homes, and a tram line that connects it to the city center.

## Triberg and the Waterfalls

An hour east of Freiburg by train or car, the small town of Triberg sits deep in a valley that seems to have been carved specifically to impress visitors. The **Triberg Waterfalls** are Germany's highest, cascading 163 meters over seven stages through dense forest. A well-maintained path (entry 8 EUR for adults) winds alongside the falls, and the sound of rushing water follows you at every turn. Early morning visits, before the tour buses arrive around 10am, offer a near-mystical experience as mist rises through shafts of sunlight.

Triberg is also ground zero for **cuckoo clocks**. The town and surrounding villages have been producing these mechanical marvels since the 1730s, and you can watch clockmakers at work in several workshops. **Haus der 1000 Uhren** (House of 1000 Clocks) is a spectacle in itself, with clocks of every size covering the walls from floor to ceiling. For a more intimate experience, visit **Schonachbach** nearby, where smaller family workshops welcome visitors and explain the mechanics behind the hand-carved birds. A quality hand-crafted cuckoo clock starts at around 180 EUR and can run into the thousands.

Just south of Triberg, keep an eye out for the two giant roadside cuckoo clocks that compete for the title of world's largest. They are gloriously absurd and absolutely worth a stop.

## Titisee and the Southern Highlands

The glacial lake of **Titisee** lies in the heart of the southern Black Forest, ringed by dark forest and backed by the gentle peak of the Hochfirst. In summer, the lake is warm enough for swimming (just barely), and you can rent pedal boats (12 EUR per hour) or rowboats (10 EUR per hour) from stands along the promenade. A lakeside path circles the entire body of water in about two hours of flat, pleasant walking.

The town of Titisee-Neustadt is touristy along its main promenade, but the surrounding landscape is magnificent. Drive or bus south to the **Feldberg**, the Black Forest's highest peak at 1,493 meters. In summer, hiking trails radiate from the summit station in every direction; in winter, a modest but enjoyable ski area operates with runs suitable for beginners and intermediates (day pass around 40 EUR).

Between Titisee and Feldberg, stop at the tiny hamlet of **Hinterzarten**, a quieter, more refined alternative to Titisee that has attracted artists and writers for over a century. The Hotel Reppert here has one of the finest terraces in the Black Forest, and a coffee and cake on their lawn overlooking the forest is a small luxury at around 10 EUR.

## Baden-Baden: Taking the Waters

At the northern edge of the Black Forest, **Baden-Baden** has been a spa destination since Roman legions discovered its thermal springs two thousand years ago. The town enjoyed its golden age in the 19th century, when European aristocracy, Russian novelists, and Parisian socialites flocked to its baths, casino, and concert halls. That legacy endures. Baden-Baden still carries itself with an elegance that feels almost theatrical, its colonnaded Kurhaus and manicured gardens immaculate to the point of obsession.

Two thermal bath complexes anchor the experience. The **Friedrichsbad** (29 EUR for 3 hours) is a neoclassical bathing palace from 1877 that follows a strict 17-step ritual of hot and cold pools, steam rooms, and scrubs. Bathing is nude and mixed-gender on most days, which surprises some visitors but is entirely normal in German bathing culture. The **Caracalla Therme** (23 EUR for 3 hours) offers a more modern, swimsuit-friendly experience with indoor and outdoor thermal pools, whirlpools, and a sauna landscape.

Beyond the baths, stroll the **Lichtentaler Allee**, a three-kilometer garden promenade along the Oos River lined with magnolias, oaks, and sculpture. The **Museum Frieder Burda** (14 EUR) houses a superb collection of modern and contemporary art in a luminous building designed by Richard Meier. And the **Casino Baden-Baden**, set inside the Kurhaus, is one of the most opulent gambling halls in the world. Even if you do not play, guided tours (7 EUR) of its gilded, chandelier-heavy interior are open to visitors.

## Hiking the Black Forest

The trail network here is staggering. Over 23,000 kilometers of marked paths crisscross the region, from gentle valley walks to multi-day ridge traversals. Three long-distance trails form the backbone.

The **Westweg** (285 km) runs the full length of the Black Forest from Pforzheim to Basel. The **Schluchtensteig** (119 km) in the south links dramatic gorges and waterfalls. And the **Zweitälersteig** (108 km) near Freiburg threads through two beautiful valleys with overnight stays in mountain huts and village guesthouses.

For day hikes, the **Wutachschlucht** (Wutach Gorge) is spectacular: a narrow canyon where the river has carved through layers of rock over millions of years, with wooden walkways bolted to cliff faces and rope-assisted sections that add a frisson of adventure. The trail from Schattenmühle to Wutachmühle takes about five hours and is moderately challenging.

Closer to Freiburg, the hike from **Schauinsland** (reachable by cable car, 13.50 EUR round trip) to **Todtnau** descends through pristine forest and Alpine-style meadows with views that stretch to the Alps on clear days.

## Where to Eat

**Gasthaus zum Raben** (Freiburg, Löwenstrasse 11): A traditional Badische Wirtschaft (tavern) serving Black Forest classics. The Rehragout (venison ragout) with Spätzle is superb, and the Flammkuchen comes crispy and generous. Mains 14 to 22 EUR.

**Café Schäfer** (Triberg, Hauptstrasse 33): The place for authentic Black Forest cake, made with Kirschwasser from local distilleries. A generous slice costs 5.50 EUR with a pot of coffee. The recipe has not changed in decades, and it does not need to.

**Colombi Hotel Restaurant Zirbelstube** (Freiburg, Rotteckring 16): For a splurge, this Michelin-recommended restaurant serves refined Badische cuisine with French influences. Tasting menus from 85 EUR.

**Schwarzwaldstube Mummelseehof** (Seebach, near the Mummelsee): A rustic mountain restaurant at a dark, mirror-still lake. The smoked Black Forest trout (16 EUR) is caught locally, and the Kirschwasser flights (8 EUR for three) let you taste the regional spirit at its finest.

**Brenners Park-Restaurant Wintergarten** (Baden-Baden, Schillerstrasse 4): Elegant dining in the legendary Brenners Park hotel. Seasonal menus 45 to 90 EUR, with impeccable service and a garden terrace overlooking the Lichtentaler Allee.

## Where to Stay

**Budget (40-75 EUR/night):** **The Black Forest Hostel** in Freiburg (Kartäuserstrasse 33) is lively and well-located, with dorm beds from 28 EUR and private doubles from 68 EUR. In the forest itself, **Naturfreundehaus Feldberg** offers simple mountain lodge accommodation from 35 EUR per person with breakfast, perfect for hikers.

**Mid-Range (90-160 EUR/night):** **Hotel Rappen** in Freiburg's Münsterplatz offers rooms with cathedral views from 110 EUR. **Parkhotel Wehrle** in Triberg (Gartenstrasse 24) is a historic four-star property with elegant rooms, a spa, and an excellent restaurant; doubles from 130 EUR including breakfast.

**Luxury (200-400 EUR/night):** **Colombi Hotel Freiburg** is the city's finest address, with impeccable rooms, a Michelin-starred restaurant, and a rooftop spa. Doubles from 240 EUR. In Baden-Baden, **Brenners Park-Hotel & Spa** is one of Europe's legendary grand hotels, set in a private park along the Oos River. Doubles start at 350 EUR, and the spa alone is worth a visit.

## Practical Tips

**KONUS card:** When you stay at a participating hotel (most in the region), you receive a free KONUS guest card that provides unlimited travel on all local buses and trains in the Black Forest. This is an extraordinary benefit. Use it liberally.

**Driving:** The Black Forest's winding roads are scenic but demanding. The **B500 Schwarzwaldhochstrasse** (Black Forest High Road) from Baden-Baden to Freudenstadt is one of Germany's most beautiful drives but requires confidence on narrow mountain roads.

**Weather:** The forest creates its own microclimate. Even in summer, temperatures drop quickly in the valleys, and afternoon thunderstorms are common in the mountains. Pack layers and rain gear regardless of the forecast.

**Language:** English is widely spoken in Freiburg and Baden-Baden but less so in smaller forest villages. A few words of German go a long way and are warmly received.

**Farm stays:** For a uniquely Black Forest experience, look into Bauernhof (farm) accommodations. Families open their working farms to guests, often with rooms in traditional farmhouses with those iconic wide roofs. Children especially love these stays, with cows, chickens, and farm-fresh breakfasts. Expect 50 to 90 EUR per night.

The Black Forest is not one place but many. It is a cathedral of trees and a sunny university city, a thundering waterfall and a silent glacial lake, a plate of cherry cake and a glass of clear Kirschwasser that burns pleasantly on the way down. It asks very little of you except time, and it gives back more than you expect. Walk deeper into the trees. The forest has been waiting.
