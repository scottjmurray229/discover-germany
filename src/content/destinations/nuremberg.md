# Nuremberg: A City That Remembers Everything

Nuremberg does not forget. This is both its burden and its gift. Standing in the courtyard of the Imperial Castle, looking out over a cityscape of soaring Gothic churches, half-timbered houses, and red sandstone towers, you are seeing a place that was rebuilt stone by stone after Allied bombing destroyed ninety percent of the Old Town in January 1945. Walk to the southern edge of the city and you will find the monumental remains of the Nazi Party Rally Grounds, preserved not as monuments but as warnings. And then return to the Old Town, find a seat at a centuries-old tavern, order a plate of tiny grilled sausages and a glass of Franconian red wine, and you will understand the other thing Nuremberg does exceptionally well: it lives, fully and unapologetically, in the present.

Bavaria's second-largest city (population 520,000) is one of Germany's most layered destinations. It was the unofficial capital of the Holy Roman Empire for centuries, the home of Albrecht Dürer and the mastersingers, the city where the first globe was made and the first pocket watch was assembled. It bore the terrible weight of being appropriated as the "City of the Nazi Party Rallies" and then hosted the tribunals that judged those crimes. And through all of this, it has maintained a distinctive Franconian identity, warmer and more grounded than stereotypical Bavarian exuberance, expressed through its food, its dialect, and its fierce local pride.

## The Imperial Castle (Kaiserburg)

The castle has commanded the sandstone ridge above Nuremberg since the 11th century, and for nearly 500 years it served as the residence of Holy Roman Emperors during their stays in the city. The complex is vast, encompassing the Sinwell Tower (climb for views), the Deep Well (a 50-meter shaft demonstrated via candle-lowering by guides), the Imperial Chapel with its unusual double-decker design separating emperor from commoners, and the Palas (Imperial Palace) with its restored imperial apartments.

Entry to the full castle complex costs 7 EUR for adults, and the audio guide is excellent. Budget at least 90 minutes. The castle gardens, cascading down the hillside toward the Old Town, are free to access and offer some of Nuremberg's finest views, particularly at sunset when the red sandstone buildings below glow as if lit from within.

## Albrecht Dürer's House

On the steep lane leading up to the castle, a handsome half-timbered house has been preserved as the home and workshop of **Albrecht Dürer** (1471-1528), the greatest artist of the Northern Renaissance. Dürer lived and worked here from 1509 until his death, producing the engravings, woodcuts, and paintings that made him famous across Europe.

The museum (6 EUR) is thoughtfully curated, with period-furnished rooms, reproductions of his printing press, and multimedia presentations that bring his working methods to life. An actress portraying Dürer's wife Agnes leads guided tours (available in English) that are engaging and surprisingly moving. Even if you arrive knowing little about Dürer, you will leave with a genuine appreciation for his genius and his deeply human personality.

## The Old Town

Nuremberg's Altstadt is divided by the Pegnitz River into two halves, the Sebalder Altstadt to the north and the Lorenzer Altstadt to the south, connected by bridges and united by the city walls that still encircle the core.

The **Hauptmarkt** (Main Market Square) is the Old Town's beating heart. The ornate Gothic **Schöner Brunnen** (Beautiful Fountain), a 19-meter spire of carved stone figures, stands at its center. Find the brass ring embedded in the fence around the fountain and spin it three times for good luck, a tradition that has polished the ring to a gleaming gold. The **Frauenkirche** (Church of Our Lady) presides over the square's eastern side, and at noon daily, mechanical figures on its facade reenact the Golden Bull ceremony in a clockwork display called the Männleinlaufen.

Cross the river via the **Museumsbrücke** to reach the **Lorenzkirche** (St. Lawrence Church), a Gothic masterwork housing Veit Stoss's extraordinary Angelic Salutation, a carved wooden rosary suspended from the ceiling, and Adam Kraft's 20-meter-tall stone tabernacle that seems to defy gravity. Entry to the church is free, and it deserves at least 30 minutes of quiet contemplation.

Wander the lanes between these anchor points and you will discover artisan workshops, independent bookshops, and hidden courtyards. The **Weissgerbergasse**, a lane of preserved tanners' houses near the river, is the Old Town's most photogenic street and home to several excellent small restaurants.

## The Nazi Documentation Center

Southeast of the Old Town, the former **Nazi Party Rally Grounds** cover an area larger than the old city itself. The sheer scale is the first thing that strikes you: the half-finished Congress Hall, modeled on the Colosseum but never completed, stretches 275 meters in length. The Zeppelin Field, where the choreographed rallies were filmed by Leni Riefenstahl, still stands with its crumbling grandstands.

The **Documentation Center** (Dokumentationszentrum), housed inside the unfinished Congress Hall, is one of Germany's most important museums. Its permanent exhibition, "Fascination and Terror," traces the rise of the Nazi party, the function of propaganda, the exploitation of Nuremberg's imperial symbolism, and the consequences of the regime. The exhibition is unflinching and meticulously documented. Plan at least two hours, and consider renting the audio guide (3 EUR) for the deeper contextual layer it provides. Entry is 8 EUR.

Nearby, the **Memorium Nuremberg Trials** at the Palace of Justice (entry 8 EUR) preserves Courtroom 600, where the International Military Tribunal tried the major Nazi war criminals in 1945-46. On days when the courtroom is not in use for active trials (it remains a functioning court), visitors can sit in the gallery where correspondents once watched history unfold.

These are not comfortable experiences, but they are essential ones. Nuremberg's willingness to confront its darkest chapter openly and honestly, rather than hiding or minimizing it, is itself a kind of moral courage that deserves respect.

## Franconian Food and Drink

Nuremberg's culinary identity is Franconian, not Bavarian, and the distinction matters. Where Bavarian cuisine runs to heavy dumplings and massive pork knuckles, Franconian cooking is more refined, with lighter sauces, river fish, and an emphasis on quality over quantity.

The **Nürnberger Bratwurst** is the city's signature dish. These tiny sausages, each about the size of a finger, are made from finely ground pork seasoned with marjoram and grilled over beechwood flames. They arrive at your table in groups of six, eight, ten, or twelve, accompanied by sauerkraut, potato salad, or simply a dollop of sharp mustard. The ritual is non-negotiable: you eat them with your fingers from the hot plate, alternating bites of sausage and sauerkraut.

**Lebkuchen** (gingerbread) is Nuremberg's other iconic food product. The city has been producing these spiced, honey-sweetened confections since the 14th century, and the best (look for Elisenlebkuchen, made with at least 25 percent nuts and no flour) are a world apart from the packaged versions sold in supermarkets. **Lebkuchen Schmidt** and **Fraunholz** offer the finest quality, with individual cakes from 3 EUR.

Franconian wine is criminally underappreciated outside the region. The vineyards around Würzburg and along the Main River produce excellent dry Silvaner and Müller-Thurgau whites served in the distinctive flat-bottomed Bocksbeutel bottles. Most wine taverns in Nuremberg pour Franconian wines by the glass from 4.50 EUR.

## Where to Eat

**Bratwursthäusle bei St. Sebald** (Rathausplatz 1): The definitive Nuremberg bratwurst experience since 1313. The tiny dining room, with its dark wood paneling and beechwood grill visible through a hatch, serves bratwurst and not much else. Six sausages with sauerkraut cost 9.50 EUR, twelve cost 13.90 EUR. The potato salad, dressed with warm broth and vinegar, is exemplary. Cash only. Expect a queue at peak times.

**Goldenes Posthorn** (Glöckleinsgasse 2): Operating since 1498, this is one of Germany's oldest wine restaurants. Albrecht Dürer himself reportedly dined here. The menu features refined Franconian cuisine: carp from local ponds (16 EUR), pork shoulder with dark beer sauce and bread dumplings (18 EUR), and seasonal dishes. The wine list is a tour of Franconia. Mains 14 to 28 EUR.

**Hexenhäusle** (Vestnertorgraben 4): A cozy, slightly quirky restaurant in a garden pavilion near the castle walls. The Franconian tapas plates (12 to 16 EUR for a selection) are ideal for sampling multiple local dishes, and the beer garden is lovely in warm weather.

**Albrecht-Dürer-Stube** (Albrecht-Dürer-Strasse 6): A traditional Franconian tavern near Dürer's house with a warm, lived-in atmosphere. Schäufele (crispy pork shoulder, the Franconian national dish) costs 15 EUR and arrives with a mountain of potato dumplings. The Franconian Sauerbraten (17 EUR) melts on the fork.

**Würzhaus** (Am Unschlittplatz 1): A more contemporary approach to Franconian cooking, with seasonal menus that reinterpret regional classics. The tasting menu (48 EUR for 5 courses) is a revelation, and the natural wine selection is one of the most interesting in the city.

## Where to Stay

**Budget (45-75 EUR/night):** **Five Reasons Hostel** (Frauentormauer 42) is a design-forward hostel in a renovated building on the old city wall. Dorm beds from 25 EUR, private doubles from 70 EUR, with a rooftop terrace and excellent common areas. **Jugendherberge Nürnberg** (Burg 2) occupies a medieval building directly inside the Imperial Castle complex, one of Germany's most dramatically located hostels. Beds from 32 EUR including breakfast.

**Mid-Range (90-160 EUR/night):** **Hotel Drei Raben** (Königstrasse 63) is a themed boutique hotel where each room tells a different Nuremberg story through art and design. Doubles from 110 EUR. **Hotel Victoria** (Königstrasse 80) is a solid, family-run four-star near the train station with comfortable rooms and excellent breakfast; doubles from 100 EUR.

**Luxury (200-350 EUR/night):** **Hotel Adina Nuremberg** (Dr.-Kurt-Schumacher-Strasse 1) offers spacious apartment-style suites with kitchenettes and a rooftop pool, from 180 EUR. **Le Méridien Grand Hotel** (Bahnhofstrasse 1-3) is a grand railway hotel reborn as a luxury property, with opulent public spaces, a spa, and doubles from 220 EUR.

## The Christkindlesmarkt

From the Friday before Advent through December 24, Nuremberg's Hauptmarkt transforms into the **Christkindlesmarkt**, arguably the world's most famous Christmas market. Over 180 wooden stalls, each draped in the traditional red-and-white striped cloth, fill the square and surrounding lanes, selling handcrafted ornaments, Rauschgoldengel (gold-foil angels, the market's symbol), Lebkuchen, roasted almonds, and Glühwein.

The market opens each year with a prologue spoken by the Christkind, a figure dressed in gold who is chosen from among Nuremberg's teenage girls in a public ceremony. The speech, delivered from the balcony of the Frauenkirche as thousands watch below, is genuinely moving, even if you do not understand every word of the German.

**Survival tips:** Visit on a Tuesday or Wednesday to avoid the worst crowds. Arrive by 4pm to see the lights come on. Bring cash, as many stalls do not accept cards. The Glühwein at the smaller Kinderweihnachtsmarkt (children's market) one block south is less crowded and just as good.

## Practical Tips

**Nuremberg Card:** The Nürnberg Card (33 EUR for 2 days) covers all public transport and free entry to over 40 museums, including the castle, Documentation Center, and Dürer's House. If you plan to visit three or more museums, it pays for itself quickly.

**Getting around:** The compact Old Town is entirely walkable. The excellent U-Bahn (subway) connects the train station to the Old Town and outlying areas including the Rally Grounds (line U11 to Doku-Zentrum station).

**Day trip to Rothenburg:** The medieval town of Rothenburg ob der Tauber is about 90 minutes from Nuremberg by regional train (one change in Steinach), making it a feasible day trip, though Rothenburg deserves an overnight stay.

**Beer culture:** While Nuremberg is in Bavaria, the local beer tradition leans toward Franconian-style Rotbier (red beer), a smoky, malty lager unique to the region. Try it at **Altstadthof** (Bergstrasse 19), which brews its own in copper kettles visible through glass walls and also operates a whisky distillery in the medieval rock-cut cellars beneath the Old Town. Tours of the cellars (8 EUR) are fascinating.

**Sensitivity:** At the Documentation Center and Trials Memorial, photography is permitted but selfies and posed photos are inappropriate. These are sites of remembrance, and visitors are expected to conduct themselves with the gravity the places demand.

Nuremberg is a city that takes you seriously. It does not simplify its history or sugarcoat its present. It serves you honest food, pours you honest wine, and tells you honest truths about what human beings are capable of, for good and for ill. That honesty, difficult and bracing and ultimately redemptive, is what makes it one of the most rewarding cities in Germany.
