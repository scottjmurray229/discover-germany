# Stuttgart: The City That Invented the Future

There is a delicious irony at the heart of Stuttgart. This is the city that gave the world the automobile. Karl Benz and Gottlieb Daimler, working independently just kilometers apart, both invented the motorcar here in 1886, and the legacy of that double stroke of genius still drives the city's economy and identity. Mercedes-Benz and Porsche are headquartered here. Engineering precision is in the municipal DNA. Yet Stuttgart is also one of Germany's greenest cities, nestled in a bowl of vine-covered hills, threaded with parks and gardens, and committed to a massive urban renewal project that is literally burying its main railway station underground to create a vast new public green space above.

Baden-Württemberg's capital (population 635,000) is not the Germany of postcards. It does not trade in medieval charm or fairy-tale castles. Instead, it offers something rarer: a prosperous, confident, culturally rich city where world-class museums sit alongside traditional wine taverns, where engineers and artists share the same neighborhoods, and where the dialect, Schwäbisch, is spoken with a pride that borders on defiance. Stuttgart takes some effort to know. It rewards that effort generously.

## The Mercedes-Benz Museum

Begin where Stuttgart's story begins: with the car. The **Mercedes-Benz Museum** (Mercedesstrasse 100) is not merely an automobile museum. It is an architectural masterpiece, a spiraling double-helix of concrete and glass that traces 140 years of automotive and social history across nine levels and 16,500 square meters.

You start at the top and descend through time. The first galleries display Karl Benz's Patent-Motorwagen of 1886, the world's first automobile, and Gottlieb Daimler's motorized carriage. From there, the exhibition unfolds chronologically: elegant pre-war limousines, the Silver Arrow racing cars that dominated Grand Prix circuits in the 1930s and 1950s, the iconic 300SL Gullwing, the vehicles that carried popes and presidents, and the engineering prototypes pointing toward an electric future.

The museum weaves social history into its automotive narrative. The section covering 1933-1945 deals honestly with the company's use of forced labor during the Nazi era. The postwar galleries show how the automobile reshaped cities, cultures, and the environment. By the time you reach the ground floor, you have experienced not just a parade of beautiful machines but a meditation on how technology shapes human life.

Entry is 16 EUR for adults (free for children under 15). The audio guide is included and excellent. Budget at least 2.5 hours, more if you are an enthusiast. The building itself, designed by UN Studio, is worth visiting for its architecture alone.

## The Porsche Museum

At the opposite end of the city in the Zuffenhausen district, the **Porsche Museum** (Porscheplatz 1) takes a different approach. Where the Mercedes museum is encyclopedic, the Porsche museum is focused and kinetic. The building, a dramatic angular structure that appears to float above its base on three slender pillars, houses roughly 80 vehicles displayed on a gleaming white floor that makes every curve and line pop.

The collection moves from Ferdinand Porsche's early designs through the evolution of the 911 (one of the most recognizable shapes in industrial design), the dominant Le Mans racing cars, and the latest hybrid hypercars. Interactive stations let you explore engine mechanics, aerodynamics, and the specific engineering challenges of each era.

Entry is 12 EUR for adults. The museum cafe on the ground level serves excellent coffee and light meals, and the adjacent Porsche experience center offers driving experiences on a handling track (from 89 EUR for 30 minutes) if you want to feel the engineering rather than just admire it.

## Schlossplatz and the City Center

Stuttgart's grand public square, **Schlossplatz**, is the city's living room. Flanked by the Baroque **Neues Schloss** (New Palace) on one side, the Renaissance **Altes Schloss** (Old Castle) on another, and the elegant **Königsbau** arcade on the third, it is a generous, graceful space where office workers eat lunch on the lawns, children splash in the fountains, and street musicians fill the air on summer evenings.

The **Altes Schloss** houses the **Württembergisches Landesmuseum** (free entry), with an excellent collection spanning Celtic gold jewelry, medieval altarpieces, and the crown jewels of Württemberg. The courtyard, with its arcaded Renaissance galleries, is one of Stuttgart's most atmospheric spaces.

From Schlossplatz, walk south along the **Königstrasse**, Stuttgart's main shopping boulevard, then turn east to reach the **Markthalle** (Market Hall), an Art Nouveau gem from 1914 where vendors sell everything from Turkish spices to Swabian cheeses, French oysters to local wines. The Markthalle is not a tourist attraction but a working market where Stuttgart's diverse population shops daily. Grab a plate at one of the stand-up counters (Turkish flatbreads from 4 EUR, Swabian Maultaschen from 5 EUR) and watch the city feed itself.

## The Staatsgalerie

The **Staatsgalerie Stuttgart** (entry 7 EUR, free on Wednesdays) is one of Germany's finest art museums, housed in a complex that pairs a stately 19th-century building with a postmodern extension by James Stirling that caused a sensation when it opened in 1984, all colored steel, pink granite, and irreverent geometry.

The collection spans 800 years, with particular strengths in the Swabian School of medieval painting, German Romantic art, French Impressionism, and 20th-century masters. The Picasso holdings are exceptional, and the Beuys room is one of the most comprehensive displays of his work outside Düsseldorf. The museum also houses a significant collection of Oskar Schlemmer, the Bauhaus master who was born in Stuttgart.

## Wine Country at the City's Edge

Stuttgart is Germany's only major city with significant vineyards within its municipal boundaries. The slopes surrounding the valley basin are covered with vines, and local Trollinger (a light red grape) and Riesling wines appear on every restaurant menu. This is not marketing spin. These are working vineyards that produce wines served in local **Besenwirtschaften** (broom taverns), seasonal establishments run by winegrowers who hang a broom above their door to signal they are open and serve only their own wine with simple food.

The **Württemberg** wine-growing region is centered on the steep, terraced slopes of the Neckar River just east of Stuttgart. Take the S-Bahn to **Untertürkheim** and walk the **Württemberger Weinwanderweg** (wine hiking trail), which climbs through vineyards with panoramic views of the city below. The trail passes several Besenwirtschaften where you can stop for a glass of Trollinger (3.50 EUR) and a plate of Swabian sausage salad (7 EUR).

For a more structured tasting experience, visit **Weingut Aldinger** in Fellbach (just northeast of Stuttgart), one of the region's top estates. Tastings cost 15 EUR for five wines and are available by appointment. Their Lemberger reds and Riesling whites rival the best in Germany.

## Cannstatter Volksfest

Stuttgart's answer to Oktoberfest, the **Cannstatter Volksfest** runs for three weeks from late September to mid-October on the Cannstatter Wasen festival grounds along the Neckar River. With nearly four million visitors annually, it is Germany's second-largest beer festival, and many Stuttgart locals will insist, with considerable justification, that it is the better one.

Seven large beer tents and numerous smaller ones serve local Swabian breweries (a Mass, or one-liter stein, costs around 13-14 EUR). The fairground is more extensive than Oktoberfest's, with thrill rides, a massive Ferris wheel, and traditional attractions. The atmosphere is boisterous but generally less overwhelming than Munich's festival.

**Reservation tip:** Book tent tables weeks in advance through the individual tent websites for Friday and Saturday evenings. Weekday afternoons and Sundays are easier to find walk-in seating. The Dinkelacker Festzelt and Schwaben Bräu tents are local favorites.

## Swabian Cuisine: Where to Eat

Stuttgart is one of Germany's best cities for eating, and the Swabian kitchen is its foundation. The essential dishes to seek out:

**Spätzle** -- hand-scraped egg noodles, irregularly shaped and slightly chewy, often served as Käsespätzle (topped with melted Emmentaler cheese and crispy fried onions, Stuttgart's answer to macaroni and cheese).

**Maultaschen** -- large pasta parcels stuffed with a mixture of minced meat, spinach, bread crumbs, and onions. Legend says they were invented by monks at the Maulbronn monastery to hide meat from God during Lent, earning them the nickname "Herrgottsbescheisserle" (little God-cheaters). They are served in broth, pan-fried with eggs, or as a salad.

**Zwiebelrostbraten** -- a thick slice of roast beef blanketed in slow-caramelized onions and rich brown gravy, always accompanied by Spätzle to soak up the sauce.

**Weinberg Stuttgarter Stäffele** (Buschlestrasse 2A): Named after Stuttgart's famous outdoor staircases (Stäffele) that connect the city's hillside neighborhoods, this restaurant serves definitive Swabian cooking in a warm, wood-paneled setting. Käsespätzle costs 14 EUR, Zwiebelrostbraten 22 EUR, and the Maultaschen in broth (10 EUR) are made fresh daily. Reserve for dinner.

**Alte Kanzlei** (Schillerplatz 5A): Set in a Renaissance building on the beautiful Schillerplatz, steps from the Altes Schloss. The menu spans traditional and modern Swabian cuisine. Maultaschen in various preparations from 12 EUR, excellent Flammkuchen from 11 EUR. The terrace is one of the best in the city.

**Weinstube Kochenbas** (Rosenstrasse 36): A genuine Swabian wine tavern with handwritten menus, ceramic wine pitchers, and an atmosphere that has not changed in decades. Linsen mit Spätzle (lentils with Spätzle and sausage, 11 EUR) is the house classic. The wine list focuses on local Württemberg producers, with glasses from 4 EUR.

**Restaurant Cube** (Kleiner Schlossplatz 1): Perched atop the Kunstmuseum Stuttgart, this glass-walled restaurant offers panoramic views over Schlossplatz and contemporary European cuisine. Lunch mains 16 to 28 EUR. Reserve a window table.

**Markthalle stands**: For quick, affordable eating, the Art Nouveau market hall offers an international food court. Turkish Lahmacun from 4.50 EUR, Swabian Maultaschen from 5 EUR, Italian antipasti plates from 8 EUR, and artisanal chocolates and baked goods to take away.

## Where to Stay

**Budget (45-75 EUR/night):** **Jugendherberge Stuttgart International** (Haußmannstrasse 27) is a modern, well-located hostel near the Eugensplatz with dorm beds from 29 EUR and private doubles from 72 EUR including breakfast. **Alex 30 Hostel** (Alexanderstrasse 30) is a smaller, more intimate option with doubles from 65 EUR in a quiet residential neighborhood.

**Mid-Range (100-170 EUR/night):** **Hotel Unger** (Kronenstrasse 17) is a family-run property near the station with stylish, recently renovated rooms from 110 EUR, including a rooftop breakfast room with city views. **Aloft Stuttgart** (Heilbronner Strasse 70) brings contemporary design, a W XYZ bar, and S-Bahn accessibility; doubles from 105 EUR.

**Luxury (220-400 EUR/night):** **Hotel am Schlossgarten** (Schillerstrasse 23) is Stuttgart's grande dame, overlooking the palace gardens with elegant rooms, a Michelin-starred restaurant, and a spa. Doubles from 230 EUR. **Jaz in the City Stuttgart** (Wolframstrasse 41) offers a more contemporary luxury experience with music-themed design, a rooftop bar, and doubles from 190 EUR.

## Beyond the Center

**Killesbergpark:** North of the center, this expansive park climbs a hillside and offers a Killesberg observation tower with panoramic views (free), a miniature railway (4 EUR), beautiful botanical gardens, and walking paths through mature forest. The park hosts the city's finest flea market on the first Saturday of each month.

**Grabkapelle on the Württemberg:** On a vineyard-covered hilltop east of the city, King Wilhelm I built this neoclassical chapel as a memorial to his beloved wife Katharina, who died young. The setting, alone on a ridge above the Neckar Valley with vineyards cascading in every direction, is profoundly romantic. Entry 4 EUR, closed Mondays.

**Esslingen am Neckar:** Just 15 minutes from Stuttgart by S-Bahn, the town of Esslingen has a beautifully preserved medieval center, Germany's oldest surviving half-timbered row of houses (dating to the 1200s), and excellent independent restaurants. Its medieval Christmas market (Mittelaltermarkt) in December, with costumed merchants and open-fire cooking, is one of the most atmospheric in Germany.

## Practical Tips

**StuttCard:** Available for 24 hours (21 EUR) or 72 hours (36 EUR), it covers public transport and free entry to most museums including both car museums. Strong value if you plan to visit multiple attractions.

**Getting around:** Stuttgart's topography (a city in a valley surrounded by hills) makes the public transport system essential. The S-Bahn, U-Bahn, and bus network is excellent. The Zahnradbahn (rack railway) from Marienplatz to Degerloch is both practical transport and a scenic ride through vineyards (included in regular transit tickets).

**The Stäffele:** Stuttgart has over 400 public staircases (Stäffele) connecting the valley floor to the hillside neighborhoods above. Walking these staircases is one of the best ways to discover the city's hidden corners. The tourist office publishes a Stäffele walking guide mapping routes through vineyards, gardens, and residential neighborhoods with panoramic views.

**Stuttgart 21:** The city center is dominated by the massive Stuttgart 21 construction project, which is replacing the terminus railway station with an underground through-station. Construction disruption in the center is significant, but the project, expected to complete by the late 2020s, will eventually create a 100-hectare park in the heart of the city.

Stuttgart does not shout for attention. It earns it. In the curve of a Silver Arrow race car, in the golden light falling through a glass of Trollinger, in the satisfying chew of hand-scraped Spätzle, in the view from a vineyard staircase at sunset when the valley fills with amber light, you find a city that has quietly mastered the art of doing things exceptionally well. The Swabians have a word for this: schaffe (to work, to create, to make). Stuttgart is what happens when an entire city takes that word to heart.
